On the Nature of Computer Science and Design

August 14th 2020

In the span of five days in the middle of August 2019 I made a change that started me down a rabbit hole I have yet to see the end of, and one that is having an overall impact on my educational career thus far. I chose to slightly alter my major from a straight computer science degree to a combined degree of computer science and design. At the time I wasn’t sure what I was getting myself into (and while that makes me technically half an art student) I can’t say I have any regrets to the decision. From it, I’ve become immersed in a world I previously knew nothing about, and in turn my curiosity and interest in computer science has only gone up.

An obvious question arises when considering the combination of a very technical major like computer science with one as abstract as design: What jobs can you get? For many the answer lies in user experience, data visualizations, and other very visual types of CS fields. I can definitely say my interests currently lie in Human-Computer Interaction. From the beginning I have always found it interesting how we not only use all the various modern technologies that surround us, but also importantly how we got to where we are now.

To me it’s a mix of people and computers. Understanding how we might interact with the software and hardware in front of us, and how it all works behind the scenes. Perhaps, in a way, HCI to me is a perfect blend of countless disciplines where there is always something new to create or learn about. This way of thinking can be applied to more than just the basic devices around us but to other fields of computer science. Personally I find programming languages incredibly fascinating for many similar reasons. By looking at the history of programming languages you can chart a path to how we ended up with the most popular languages and practices of today.

In its most abstract sense I relish the “how” and “why”. How we got to where we are now, why are things done the way they are, and based on that information how can it be improved going forwards? I read a quote somewhere that said “Simplicity is an art of iteration” and that applies to code as much as it does to design. Improvements require looking back on the past and learning from it. Maybe this is the reason I enjoy HCI. To be able to ask these abstract questions and continue to learn.

Of course where does design fit into all of this? A simple answer lies in the interface of whatever is being made or anything related to the visuals of the project. Although other than that I believe there are some abstract ideas from design that can be applied to computer science to create a unique blend of these two different worlds. So far my design classes have left me looking closer at the world around me attempting to understand it more. Just as I find myself staring at every logo as I walk around Boston, I can’t help but take a deeper look at the various software practices and principles attempting to understand them more. There is no one all encompassing definition of what design is, but it’s an area where nothing is set in stone and is always changing. Something that can be very much said about software. Now with a year of coursework done so far I think I can say I made the right decision to go down this unique path of computer science. I don’t know what the future will hold or how my interests may change, but for now I enjoy where the road is taking.

Till Next Time,
-Matt
